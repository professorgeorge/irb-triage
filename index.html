Released April 01, 2026
Researchers, Reviewers, Editors
About this tool

The IRB Triage is a self-contained, offline tool for thinking clearly about research ethics review. Pick a role below, answer the questions, and the tool returns a structured assessment: the documents you will likely need, the red flags worth surfacing, the questions worth raising, and the timelines to plan for.

Four working modes cover the full lifecycle of a study. A researcher figuring out which review track applies. A committee member (IRB, REC, HREC, IEC) working through the review criteria. A peer reviewer or editor checking whether a manuscript’s ethics statement holds up. A principal investigator with an approved study deciding whether a change needs an amendment, a prompt report, or both. The recommendation engine draws on the Common Rule, ICMR guidelines, TCPS 2, the National Statement, the NHC and MOST measures, the CEP/CONEP system, GDPR, the Declaration of Helsinki, ICMJE, and COPE, and translates them into plain working language.

A note from the author

Much of my work has been at institutions that serve communities the rest of academia tends to underestimate. Researchers there face the same hard questions as their better-resourced peers, with thinner support and longer waits. The reviewers and editors evaluating their work sometimes lack the specialised training that makes a quick read possible. This tool is for both. It will not replace an ethics committee, and does not pretend to. What it tries to do is make the conversation that produces good ethics review a little more accessible, a little better structured, and a little less intimidating. If it spares one careful researcher a misstep, or one good study an avoidable delay, it has earned its keep.

Most researchers don’t need a verdict; they need to know which review track they’re heading toward so they can plan timelines and prepare documents. Answer the questions below. The right panel narrows in as you go. The final report includes likely documents, red flags, and questions to bring to the IRB office.

Q1

Where is the institution, and where will the work happen?

Each country has its own framework. The recommendation engine adjusts language and citations based on this answer.

Why does this matter

Most frameworks share core principles (informed consent, risk minimisation, vulnerable population protections) but diverge on procedure. In the US, even “exempt” research usually requires the IRB to make the exemption determination; you don’t self-exempt. In the UK, the HRA decides whether their approval is required for NHS research, but university research often runs through institutional RECs. Canada’s TCPS 2 has a distinct chapter on Indigenous research. India’s ICMR distinguishes social/behavioural from biomedical pathways, and IECs reviewing biomedical research must register with DHR via the Naitik portal. China now operates two parallel 2023 frameworks (NHC for biomedical, MOST for broader science and AI). Brazil tightened its system with Lei 14.874/2024.

Q2

What is the primary purpose of this work?

This question alone often settles things. If the work isn’t “research” under the regulatory definition, IRB review may not apply at all (though the institution may still want to know).

Q3

What is the data source?

“Are humans involved” sounds simple. The wrinkle is secondary data; nobody is being interviewed, but the data still came from people.

Q4

What activities are involved? (select all)

Each activity carries different risk profiles and consent requirements. Selecting many isn’t bad; it just sharpens the recommendation.

Q5

Population characteristics (select all that apply)

Frameworks worldwide treat certain groups as needing extra protection. If any apply, expect closer scrutiny and additional consent procedures.

Q6

How identifiable is the data?

Identifiability often determines whether a study can clear the exempt threshold. Even “de-identified” data can re-identify under the wrong conditions.

Q7

Topic sensitivity (select all)

Sensitive topics combined with identifiable data is the combination that pushes most studies into full board review.

Q8

Best estimate of risk to participants

“Minimal risk” has a specific regulatory meaning: probability and magnitude of harm no greater than ordinarily encountered in daily life or routine examinations.

P1

What kind of change happened?

Different changes carry different obligations. Some need an amendment before you can implement them, some need a report after the fact, some need both.

P2

Has the change been implemented yet?

Most frameworks require IRB approval before implementing protocol changes, with a narrow exception for changes needed to eliminate immediate hazard.

P3

Does it affect risk to participants?

Risk-relevant changes are reviewed differently from purely administrative ones.

Important This is a self-assessment tool. It can prepare you for a smarter conversation with an IRB, REC, HREC, IEC, or equivalent body. It cannot replace one. Final determination rests with the institutional ethics body.

Reference shelf · Foundational documents

Belmont Report 1979

The three foundational principles for US human subjects research: respect for persons, beneficence, justice. Underlies the Common Rule.

Declaration of Helsinki WMA

World Medical Association statement on ethics in medical research; the international gold standard most journals expect biomedical research to follow. Most recent revision: 2024.

CIOMS Guidelines 2016

Council for International Organizations of Medical Sciences. Health-related research in low-resource settings, vulnerable populations, biobanking, cluster trials.

Common Rule 45 CFR 46

US federal policy on human subjects protection. Eight exempt categories under \u00a746.104(d)(1)\u2013(8). Revised 2018, effective January 21, 2019.

Nuremberg Code 1947

Ten principles arising from the Nuremberg trials. Voluntary consent is principle one. Historical bedrock of all research ethics frameworks.

ICMJE Recommendations Journals

International Committee of Medical Journal Editors. Defines what journals expect in ethics statements, consent disclosures, and authorship of submitted manuscripts.

COPE Guidelines Editors

Committee on Publication Ethics. Flowcharts for handling suspected research misconduct, missing ethics approval, and post-publication concerns.

Common consent elements \u00a746.116

Eight basic elements (purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, alternatives, confidentiality, compensation, contacts, voluntary participation) plus additional elements when applicable.

Glossary

Generalizable Knowledge
Findings intended to be applied beyond the immediate program or setting. The intent to generalize is what often distinguishes "research" from quality improvement.
Minimal Risk \u00a746.102
Probability and magnitude of harm no greater than what one would encounter in daily life or during routine physical or psychological examinations.
Vulnerable Population
Groups whose capacity to give voluntary, informed consent may be limited or compromised: children, prisoners, pregnant people, those with cognitive impairment, economically dependent groups.
Identifiable Private Information
Data that includes identifiers, or where identity could be readily ascertained from the data itself or by linking to other accessible data.
Re-identification Risk
The chance that supposedly de-identified or anonymous data can be linked back to specific individuals, often by combining with other available datasets.
Limited IRB Review 2018 Rule
A streamlined review for certain exempt categories (especially \u00a746.104(d)(2)(iii), (3), (7), (8)) where the IRB confirms specific privacy and confidentiality safeguards are met.
Broad Consent \u00a746.116(d)
Consent for storage, maintenance, and secondary research use of identifiable information or biospecimens; specific elements required, distinct from study-specific consent.
Waiver of Consent
An IRB-granted exception to the consent requirement, allowed only when the research could not practicably be done otherwise and risks are minimal.
Algorithmic Harm
Risks unique to AI/ML systems: bias amplification, opacity in decision-making, automation of consequential judgments, training data leakage.
Model Card / Datasheet
Standardised documentation for an AI model or training dataset, intended to surface intended uses, limitations, and ethical considerations.
Data Minimisation GDPR Art. 5
The principle that personal data collected should be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the purposes; foundational across modern privacy frameworks.
Contextual Integrity
The norm that information flows are appropriate when they match the norms of the context in which the information was originally shared. Key concept for online and social media research.
Certificate of Confidentiality US
NIH-issued protection allowing researchers to refuse compelled disclosure of identifying information in legal proceedings. Other jurisdictions have analogous protections.
Continuing Review
Periodic re-approval of an active study, traditionally annual; often eliminated for minimal-risk studies under the 2018 Common Rule revisions.
Data Safety Monitoring Board DSMB
Independent group monitoring participant safety and trial integrity in higher-risk biomedical studies; required for many phase III trials.
HRA / HREC / REC / REB / IEC Various
Research ethics review bodies by region: Health Research Authority (UK), Human Research Ethics Committee (Australia), Research Ethics Committee (UK universities), Research Ethics Board (Canada), Institutional Ethics Committee (India).
